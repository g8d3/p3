[BODY]
Let’s talk architecture first.
Inworld has a character brain.
That brain tracks the conversation.
It knows if the NPC is losing an argument.
It knows if the player is being rude.
When you hit “speak”, the brain sends a data bundle.
Text + Emotion + Intent + Style.
Standard TTS just reads the sentence.
Inworld TTS *performs* it.
This is the single biggest leap in NPC dialogue tech.

[EXAMPLE]
Scenario: A tavern brawl.
NPC: The nervous bartender.
Setup 1: Standard TTS.
Input: “Hey! Take it outside before I call the guards!”
Output: Clean. Slightly annoyed. Flat.
Setup 2: Inworld TTS.
Backstory: Ex-adventurer. Hates violence. Scared of a lawsuit.
Context: The player threw the first punch.
Response: *Voice raises an octave.* “HEY! Knock it off!”
*Voice drops, trembles slightly.* “…Take it outside.”
*Frustrated exhale.* “Before I… before I call the guards.”
It didn’t read the line. It *reacted*.
The TTS engine applied dynamic prosody.
It changed the rhythm.
This is the secret sauce.

[BODY2]
Let’s talk practicalities.
Latency is the killer of conversation.
Lowest latency mode? Sub 500ms. Instant.
High quality mode? A bit heavier.
The real magic is emotional mapping.
You can define parameters. “Anger: High.”
Or you can let the brain decide.
I found letting the brain decide gave the most natural results.
It doesn’t scream every angry line.
It gets quiet. It gets scary.
This is intelligent audio.

[BODY3]
Okay. Opinion time.
Is this the best raw TTS engine?
ElevenLabs still rules for pure voice cloning.
PlayHT still wins for voice variety.
But if you need a character that *lives and breathes*?
The gap is enormous.
Inworld wins by a landslide.
The trade off is ecosystem lock-in.
You cannot buy the voice engine standalone.
You buy the whole character suite.
This is perfect for games.
This is restrictive for animatics or ads.
Know your use case.
